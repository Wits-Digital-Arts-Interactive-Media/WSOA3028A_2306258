Blog 11: Decolonising Technology through code

Engaging with the concept of decoloniality in coding requires acknowledging and confronting the potential for technology to uphold or confront colonial influences and hierarchies. Here is an exploration of how our coding practices can play a part in promoting decoloniality.

Representation and Diversity

Code encourages diversity and representation in technology, which helps advance decolonization. This includes:

  • Inclusive Design: Ensuring that platforms and software are usable and accessible by individuals from a variety of cultural backgrounds, especially those who have been historically marginalized or excluded.
  • Multilingual Support: Creating applications that can support languages other than the mainstream ones, such as dialects and native tongues.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Taking into account subtle cultural differences while developing algorithms and user interfaces to prevent the reinforcement of biases or stereotypes.

Open Access and Knowledge Sharing

Encouraging accessibility to information and technology for everybody, irrespective of geography or financial standing, is another aspect of decolonizing through code:

  • Open Source Initiatives: Supporting open-source initiatives that facilitate international cooperation and lessen reliance on proprietary technologies controlled by a select few.
  • Educational Resources: Producing and disseminating materials that enable local communities to design and implement their own technology solutions.

Ethical Data Practices

To decolonize technology, it is imperative to address data colonialism:

  • Data Sovereignty: Respecting data ownership and privacy rights, particularly in situations where there has been past exploitation.
  • Fair Algorithms: Creating algorithms that prioritize openness and fairness, thereby preventing the perpetuation of current power disparities.

Community Engagement and Empowerment

Enabling local communities to interact with and influence technology:

  • Participatory Design: Involving communities in the design and development process to ensure that technologies are developed to fit their real needs.
  • Building Community Capacity: Providing communities with the tools and training they need to use technology and adapt it to fit their own priorities.

Example: Indigenous Knowledge Systems

Creating software to convey and preserve indigenous knowledge systems is an example of decolonial coding. This could entail setting up venues for cultural activities, oral histories, and traditional ecological knowledge. These programs honor the sovereignty of indigenous peoples over their knowledge while utilizing technology to guarantee its continuity and preservation.

We can help decolonize technology by intentionally incorporating the values of equity, inclusivity, and respect for many cultures into the way we code. This strategy not only lessens harm but also encourages empowerment and innovation among international communities, advancing a more just and equitable technological environment.
